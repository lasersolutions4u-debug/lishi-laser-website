How Fabrication Shops Save 33% on Gas Costs with Mixed Gas Technology

Published: April 26, 2026 10 min read

Every fabrication shop owner knows the feeling: you look at the monthly nitrogen bill and wonder if there's a better way. For one medium-sized sheet metal fabricator — let's call them Midwest Metal Works — that question led to a 33% reduction in nitrogen costs, doubled cutting output, and a 3-month ROI on their gas mixing equipment. Here's exactly how it happened, with real numbers you can use to estimate your own savings.

The Starting Point: A Typical High-Volume Shop

Midwest Metal Works runs two 20KW fiber laser cutting machines processing primarily carbon steel — from 6mm structural brackets to 20mm machine frames. Before switching to mixed gas, their setup looked like this:

Average cutting speed (10mm CS)~4 m/min (pure N₂)
Beam-on time~60% (rest is loading/piercing/idle)
Deburring labor2 workers × 4 hours/day on burred parts
Monthly deburring cost~$1,200

The shop's biggest pain points were familiar to anyone running pure N₂ on carbon steel: burrs on plates 8mm and thicker, parts that needed grinding before they could ship, and nitrogen bills that never seemed to stop climbing.

The Switch: Installing Mixed Gas

The shop installed a LISHI LASER Mixed Gas Device in a one-to-two configuration — one mixing station feeding both 20KW lasers simultaneously. The installation took one day and required no modifications to the laser machines themselves.

The device was set to a 95% N₂ / 5% O₂ ratio — the standard starting point for carbon steel. After two weeks of parameter tuning (mainly adjusting cutting speeds upward as operators gained confidence in the edge quality), the shop locked in their new production parameters.

The Doubled Output Story

The shop didn't actually increase their working hours. Output doubled because cutting at 12–14 m/min instead of 4 m/min means each sheet spends far less time under the laser. A job that used to take 4 hours of beam-on time now takes about 1.3 hours. The "extra" capacity absorbs more jobs — or lets the shop take on work they previously had to turn down.

Why the Savings Are So Large

Three compounding effects drive the economics:

1. Less nitrogen consumed per part

Mixed gas replaces ~30% of the N₂ flow with far cheaper O₂. But more importantly, because the laser cuts 3× faster, it spends less total time cutting each part. Less cutting time = less gas flowing. This is the compounding effect most shops underestimate.

2. Deburring eliminated entirely

Before mixed gas, about 40% of the shop's carbon steel parts needed some degree of secondary edge work — grinding off burrs, cleaning oxidation, or polishing for customer acceptance. Two workers spent a combined 8 hours per day on this. With mixed gas producing burr-free, silver-white edges, that labor line item went to zero.

3. Higher throughput without more machines

Cutting 3× faster means the shop's existing laser capacity effectively tripled. They didn't need to buy a third laser to double their output — they just made their existing machines far more productive. This is the least visible but most valuable economic benefit.

Is This Typical? What to Expect in Your Shop

Midwest Metal Works saw results on the higher end of the typical range because they cut primarily carbon steel in the 6–20mm thickness range — the sweet spot for mixed gas. Your results will vary based on:

  • Material mix: Shops cutting mostly carbon steel see the biggest gains. Stainless steel and aluminum see speed improvements but less dramatic gas savings.
  • Thickness range: The speed multiplier is highest on thin-to-medium plate (6–16mm). On 30mm+ plate, the speed gain is still significant (~3×) but the absolute time saved per sheet is smaller.
  • Current gas setup: Shops switching from pure N₂ see the biggest cost reduction. Shops switching from O₂ see the biggest speed improvement.
  • Beam-on percentage: Higher utilization amplifies both gas savings and throughput gains. A shop already running at 80% beam-on will save more gas than one at 40%.

As a rough rule of thumb for a single 20KW laser cutting carbon steel 8 hours/day: expect $20,000–$30,000/year in combined gas savings, labor reduction, and maintenance elimination. The equipment investment typically pays back in 3–6 months.
